# 04. Risk Management And Position Sizing

## Why Risk Management Matters

Risk management is the foundation of trading. A trader can have a good strategy and still lose money if risk is not controlled.

The goal is not to win every trade. The goal is to protect capital and survive long enough to improve.

## Main Risk Rule

Risk only a small fixed percentage of capital on each trade.

Common beginner range:

- 0.5% per trade
- 1% per trade
- Maximum 2% only after experience

## What Is Stop Loss?

Stop loss is the price level where the trader exits if the trade goes wrong.

Stop loss is not failure. It is protection.

## What Is Target?

Target is the price level where the trader books profit.

## Risk-Reward Ratio

Risk-reward compares possible loss with possible profit.

Example:

- Risk: Rs. 1,000
- Reward: Rs. 2,000
- Risk-reward: 1:2

If a trader keeps 1:2 risk-reward, the trader does not need to win every trade to be profitable.

## Position Sizing Formula

Position size = Amount willing to risk / Stop loss distance

Example:

- Capital: Rs. 50,000
- Risk per trade: 1% = Rs. 500
- Entry: 100
- Stop loss: 95
- Stop distance: 5
- Quantity = 500 / 5 = 100 shares

## Daily Loss Limit

A trader should set a maximum daily loss.

Example:

- If daily loss reaches 2% of capital, stop trading for the day.

This prevents emotional damage and revenge trading.

## Weekly Loss Limit

If a trader loses too much in a week, the trader should reduce size or pause.

Example:

- If weekly loss reaches 5%, stop trading and review all trades.

## Risk Mistakes

- Trading without stop loss
- Increasing quantity after loss
- Averaging a losing intraday trade without plan
- Using full capital in one trade
- Holding loss because of hope
- Booking small profit but allowing large loss

## Before Entering Any Trade

Ask:

1. Where is my entry?
2. Where is my stop loss?
3. Where is my target?
4. How much can I lose?
5. Is the reward worth the risk?
6. If this trade loses, will my account still be safe?

## Key Lesson

A professional trader thinks about loss before profit. If risk is not clear, there is no trade.
